Essential English Punctuation and Grammar

Part 3. Colons & Semicolons

For special occasions.

Colons

Colons are used in the following ways: to introduce a list, a direct quote, or an explanation/example.

Jane Austen published four novels in her lifetime : Sense and Sensibility, Pride and Prejudice, Mansfield Park, and Emma.

Colons must follow an independent clause.

Hamlet began his soliloquy boldly : "To be, or not to be: that is the question."

Check that double colon! In Shakespeare's famous line, Hamlet clarifies that his first statement is an existential question. You'll rarely see the third case (introduce an explanation or example) on the ACT/SAT. Just make sure the clause preceding a colon can stand on its own.

Even Muffy's friends acknowledged that most people used one word to describe her : snob.


Semicolons

Semicolons connect independent clauses, and indicates a stronger relationship between them than a period. They are often used in place of a comma-conjunction.

The Mantis shrimp packs a deadly punch; its claws can strike prey with 1,500 Newtons of force and create a shockwave of boiling cavitation bubbles.
A. NO CHANGE
B. punch. Because its
C. punch, its
D. punch, but its

Choice B creates a dependent clause after the period; otherwise, a period would be a fine choice. On the ACT/SAT, you won't be asked to pick between two acceptable options - one will always be better suited to the sentence.

Choice C creates a comma splice. We'd need a conjunction to link these independent clauses together.

TIP: Sometimes the test makers like to play with conjunctions. Choice D, in this case, picked a conjunction that shows a contrast. However, the clause starting with "its claws" doesn't offer contrasting information; it explains why the Mantis shrimp's punch is deadly.

With two closely related clauses, Choice A is our best answer.

CORRECT! Choice A and the semicolon correctly join two closely related clauses.
