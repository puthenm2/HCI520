Essential English Punctuation and Grammar

Part 2. The Comma

For lists, pauses, and general sentence organization.

Serial Commas

Also known as the Oxford Comma, these commas are used after the second to last item in a list of 3+ items, before the and/or. Many consider this a stylistic choice, meaning serial commas aren't required to get your point across. On the ACT and SAT, however, you're expected to include them. You never know what kinds of misunderstandings you can create without them!

How does this sentence look without commas?

I find inspiration in cooking my family and my pets.

I'm doing what now? Quick, throw in the serial comma!

I find inspiration in cooking, my family, and my pets.

Phew! This makes me look way less dangerous. Now, it's clear I'm just inspired by a list of three things.

With Adjectives

When describing a word with two or more adjectives, use commas to separate them.

The Midwest is famous for its sprawling , corn fields.

Why isn't the sentence above a good example of this rule? You can think of a "corn field" as a distinct entity - in other words, "corn" is not an adjective describing "fields."

There's a lot of vast , empty , open space in the Midwest.

Here, "space" is described as vast and empty and open.

We use conjunctions to connect words, and they're especially useful for stitching together clauses and phrases.

My mother's birthday is the week before Mother's Day so we do a big celebration in-between.
A. NO CHANGE
B. Day, so we
C. Day so, we
D. Day, so we,

We need a comma somewhere, but you should never pick Choice C. Commas don't come after conjunctions.

Additionally, the test makers like to sneak in commas at the end of answer choices - so beware the trap of Choice D that puts a comma after "we." Choice B correctly displays a comma-conjunction pair.

CORRECT! Put your commas before your conjunctions.


Introductory Words

Use a comma when you begin with a transition or other introductory words.

Well , I suppose we could make room in our schedule for a meeting next week.


Truth be told , we've never interviewed anyone quite so famous.


Interrupters & Parentheticals

As discussed in the Intervening Phrase/Clause section of Lesson 1, you will find extra pieces of information that can be taken out, while keeping the meaning of a sentence intact.

For example:

Sticking with the violin through high school, I believe, taught me the value of self-discipline.

The phrase "I believe" is an interrupter, so it should be set off, or bracketed, by commas. In other words, the phrase is nonessential. So, what happens when a phrase or clause is essential to understanding the meaning of the sentence? See below:

The woman who left her purse is coming back for it.

Take out the underlined information and the sentence becomes:

The woman is coming back for it.

While not strictly incorrect, the sentence is confusing - we don't know what "it" is.

Beware the Comma Splice

When stitching together complex sentences with many different clauses, you'll want to be careful with your comma usage. A comma splice happens when you try to combine independent clauses by just using a flimsy comma. This can be fixed in three ways:

The professor hosted a review session for the last class before the exam, several students had neglected to show up.

With a comma-conjunction:

The professor hosted a review session for the last class before the exam , but several students had neglected to show up.

With a semicolon:

The professor hosted a review session for the last class before the exam ; several students had neglected to show up.

With a period:

The professor hosted a review session for the last class before the exam . Several students had neglected to show up.

We'll expand on the semicolon in Lesson 3 and the period in Lesson 5. Commas are versatile and incredibly useful punctuation marks when it comes to organization and making sentences readable. Just make sure you're using them deliberately, following the rules, and taking a moment to decide, "Do I really need a pause here?"
